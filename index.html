Soccer Referee

I got my very first job when I was thirteen. After completing a training course, I began refereeing soccer games for the local children's league. This is still quite possibly the worst job I've ever had. Some parents take their children's sports far too seriously...

Fall Festival Staff

Refereeing didn't last long, and I soon moved on to the local orchard. Every Autumn they held a big Fall Festival, with a pumpkin patch, hay-rides, game booths, apple-picking, and a haunted house. I was on the staff for this and spent most of my time running the hay-bale maze, the apple slingshot, and the scarecrow-making booth. I enjoyed working outside and getting a weekly paycheck as a middle-schooler was great, as were the apple-cider doughnuts I ate all Fall!

Stock Clerk

When I turned 16, I got my first year-round job working as a stock clerk at the liquor store my neighbor owned. I worked on "Beer Crew" at the store, which is one of the largest in the country. My duties included stocking shelves and coolers, answering customer questions, and moving cases and kegs to customers' cars. I worked here all throughout high school and came back a couple times between college semesters. I moved to cashier after I turned 18, and preferred that to the manual labor of "Beer Crew," though I did miss the tips I would get for doing "Carry-Outs" to customers cars.

Factory Worker

I spent the Summer after my Freshman year of college working at a W.L. Gore factory. The company makes a variety of products, but the area I worked in produced cables for missiles and rocket ships. Even though I was hired as a worker in the stockroom, I was made to sign a government waiver saying I would not tell anyone outside of the plant about what I did at work. After a couple weeks of filling orders for 100's of O-rings and other tiny parts, I was moved to the "Clean Room," where workers produced cables in a sterile enviornment, wearing lab coats and gloves. I much preferred soldering and using lazers to cut wire jackets over the tedious, boring work of the stockroom.

HVAC Installer

After Sophomore year of college, I was hired by a friend's dad who owned a Heating and Air-Conditioning business. I installed duct systems in new houses and installed new furnaces/AC-units in customers' homes. I decided to take a semester off from school that Fall and worked with the company for about 8 months. I returned to work for the company between semesters, as well as for a time after graduation. While I did hate working in 110-degree attics and dirty, musty crawl spaces, I learned so many useful skills on this job, like carpentry, electric, and plumbing. Also, the friend whose dad I worked for soon became my girlfriend, and eventually became my wife!

Campus Dining Hall Staff

After my semester off, I transferred to a college in the midwest. While attending school there, I was hired by the campus' dining hall as a dishwasher. My roommate and I worked the same dishwashing shift for a time, which made the work more fun. Eventually I was asked to start serving food, then immediately moved to the grill, where I cooked burgers, grilled cheese sandwiches, chicken strips, and french fries as diners ordered. This job (thankfully) didn't last too long, as a better opportunity soon presented itself.

Recording Studio Manager

While taking Studio Recording classes in college, my professor asked me to work as a manager for the school's recording studios. I was happy to leave my job in the dining hall and work in an environment I really enjoyed. I was charged with managing the studio-time schedule, letting students into the studios for their scheduled times, checking out microphones and other equipment, and assisting students when they needed help with projects. I was also asked to re-wire the main studio after renovations were complete. This job gave me extra practice in my area of study, and I was thrilled to have this opportunity.

Direct Support Professional

After graduation, my then-fiance and I moved to Saint Louis. I got a job with a non-profit organization that assisted people with developmental disabilities. I worked as support staff in the home of six disabled men. This remains one of the most rewarding, and one of the most challenging experiences of my life. The men were often very grateful for the assistance and I formed friendships with most of them. I got to take residents to the movies, restaurants, and sporting events. There were also very hard times, however, like when a resident would get angry or violent, or when someone required assistance in the bathroom. I worked here for over a year, until I was offered a job that paid much better.

Live Sound Engineer

While working in the group home, I was offered a job running sound at a brand new bar owned by a fellow alumni of my college. I started doing this on the side as a night job a couple nights a week. I set up microphones and ran the sound board for live concerts. Again, I loved working in this field and getting more experience. The downside, however, was that I sat through a lot of terrible sets by inexperienced local bands.

Bartender

After a few months of running live sound at the bar/venue, the owner/operators decided to hire their first bartender. I told them I was interested, and soon began learning the trade. It was a good fit, and I've now been working behind bars for eight years. I've since bounced around a bit, and worked at a variety of types of establishments, from South City dives, to tourist-y bars at the beach, to fancy cocktail bars. I've had a knowledge of and appreciation for alcohol since working at the liquor store in high school, and I've also really enjoyed the social aspect of bartending. I've made many good friends through my time bartending, and I will certainly miss it when I move on.

The Future

Though I've loved my years as a bartender, I'm ready to move on to a new career. I've really been enjoying learning to code in LaunchCode and I look forward to starting a career in programming. I've been flying through the course-work, and I actually look forward to getting back to work on my assignments. I think this industry is an excellent fit for me, and I sometimes wish I had applied to LaunchCode years ago. I look forward to continuing to learn and getting a job I can love for years to come.
